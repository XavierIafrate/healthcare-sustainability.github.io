About the Author

Executive Summary

This report aims to introduce and contextualise the issue of laboratory sustainability in the UK. By surveying the current technologies and initiatives in the UK and beyond, the report shows that there is a slowly growing lab sustainability movement in the UK that requires accelerating.

By learning from some of the world’s leading laboratories and healthcare institutions in the USA and implementing changes quickly (within the next 5 years), the UK could drastically reduce its emissions relating to scientific and healthcare research. In turn, this would help the UK meet its net-zero goals.

This report clearly identifies concrete actions that the UK’s research agencies, funding bodies and researchers/healthcare providers need to take to support the transition to sustainable laboratory and healthcare practices. These recommendations are concisely summarised in the conclusions. Please note that this is an abridged version of the report for readability. For the full report, please visit the Churchill Fellowship website.

What's the problem?

Twenty years ago, few scientists – if any – cared about the sustainability of their laboratory practices.[1] The most recent data available suggest that, in 2018, almost £4.8 billion was spent on health and medical research.[2] This figure grows year on year. Most scientists would like to think that they are using grant money efficiently and that the benefits of their academic research outweigh any possible deleterious effects on the environment. Unfortunately, this is not always true. As the world’s population surpasses 8 billion and our energy requirements continue to consume an unsustainable amount of fossil fuels, it is important that everybody reviews their environmental impact. As scientists and healthcare workers, we are not simply pardoned because our work serves society – our research consumes a disproportionate amount of energy and generates huge volumes of waste.

"healthcare research is responsible for 100 megatonnes of carbon dioxide annually"

Scientists have a far greater impact on the environment than the average citizen. A recent report from the Sustainable Healthcare Coalition estimated that healthcare research is responsible for 100 megatonnes of carbon dioxide equivalent (CO2e) emissions annually.[3] Considering that there are 8.8 million scientists globally, and that approximately 12% of these are healthcare researchers, this means that the average healthcare scientist could have a carbon footprint of 100 tonnes per year.[4] That’s a staggering 2,000 times more than the average Bangladeshi citizen.[5] Considering the challenges that Bangladesh and other developing nations face with respect to the rising sea levels caused by climate change, this statistic crystallises, firstly, the injustice of the climate emergency, and secondly, that we scientists are more responsible for this inequality than we may have liked to think.

The NHS is responsible for a staggering 4% of the UK’s carbon emissions

and is the largest plastics user in all of Europe.[6] There is no reason why we can’t attempt to reduce this impact while simultaneously upholding excellent levels of care. Good quality healthcare and reproducible science relies upon the provision of sterile equipment and consistent conditions. These typically take the form of single-use plastics and energy-guzzling heating, ventilation and air conditioning (HVAC) equipment.

Due to the complexity of the research ecosystem, the identification and quantification of areas for improvement is challenging. Meanwhile, funding bodies are slowly waking up to the idea that reducing inefficiencies in research is important from both a stakeholder and a financial perspective: grant holders need to learn to do more with less to (1) protect the environment and (2) ensure that funds are used effectively. In fact, the Medical Research Council recently announced a £1 million grant call seeking solutions to the problem of sustainability in life sciences and medical practice.[7] However, at present there are no tangible incentives for science to be practised more sustainably.

The UK is planning to double scientific research investment – while simultaneously committing to a zero-carbon vision for 2050.[8] As scientists and as stakeholders in planet Earth, how can we meet this challenge within our research institutions?

Systemic change is required.

In this report, I explain the challenges we face in our healthcare and research spaces, and explore innovative, translatable solutions to these problems from some of the world’s most forward-thinking organisations. Finally, I conclude with some recommendations for stakeholders in science and healthcare to allow our scientific community to reach a more sustainable future.

Facilities and how people use them.

Facilities

Research facilities and the energy that powers them are commonly referred to as scope 1 and scope 2 emissions, respectively.[9] Scope 1 and 2 emissions can also be thought of as ‘direct’ emissions – those that are under the control of the organisation in question, as opposed to ‘indirect’ scope 3 emissions which occur as a consequence of other companies’ activities and are not controlled by the organisation. Research facilities are estimated to use 10 times more power per unit area than the average family home. In laboratories, HVAC equipment consumes between 40% and 70% of that energy.[10] Therefore, HVAC equipment is clearly a priority target for energy-saving measures. Autoclaves and ultra-low temperature (ULT) freezers are other obvious targets. See Table 1 for a comparison of the relative energy demands of laboratory equipment. The future of research involves more automation and computing power – these machines will further add to the energy requirements of hospitals and research centres.

Since lab equipment is usually shared between multiple groups, these facilities are difficult to manage efficiently. However, there are also more ubiquitous and less obvious energy (and water) vampires, such as PCR machines, incubators, imaging devices, condensers and water baths, et cetera. These all provide opportunities for energy and water savings. However, whilst retrofits and new equipment can increase the efficiency of laboratory spaces, a lot can be achieved by changing how people use their facilities and equipment. To do this, we need to a) make people aware of the issues, and b) help them change their behaviour.

Behaviour

Every day, scientists make decisions about their research which inadvertently affect the environment. For instance, the average scientist would ordinarily not think twice about putting a thermocycler on overnight with a 4 oC hold, leaving the fume hood sash half open, or rummaging around in the ULT freezer for a few minutes too long. These unsustainable practices tend to be well-established and highly ingrained.

To change people’s behaviours, one first must understand their motivations. A lab manager might think that applying electricity shut-off times to lab equipment would be an effective solution to ensure equipment does not run overnight. However, rules such as this can be surprisingly upsetting for researchers, who are often under a lot of pressure and feel constrained by time. An overnight shutdown is not an empathetic solution to this problem because running a machine or experiment overnight is one of the most efficient uses of a researcher’s time. However, if the lab manager could implement such a scheme that had an opt-out system, then this would perhaps solve everyone’s problems. Different researchers and practitioners have unique requirements, so it is crucial that they are involved in decision-making.

Anecdotally, implementing sustainable science practices increased the amount of time I had to spend on a given task. Why? Washing reusable glassware, separating plasticware and taking waste to multiple different bins – the list of best practices goes on and includes (but is not limited to):

  • forward planning to ensure that equipment (e.g. dishwashers, autoclaves) is used efficiently
  • consolidating orders to ensure that procurement is as efficient as possible (see Supply Chain)
  • ensuring that inventories of fridge/freezer/cold room contents are created and kept up to date so that the doors are opened for as short a time as possible (therefore people are no longer rummaging around for missing/misplaced/mislabelled samples unnecessarily)
  • adjusting the setpoint of ULT freezers from -80 oC to -70 oC where necessary (reducing compressor cycles)
  • powering down equipment at the plug after use or fitting power timers on appropriate equipment
  • shutting down fume hoods in between use
  • not storing chemicals and equipment in fume hoods
  • recycling as much waste as possible (see Supply Chain)
These additional tasks, while menial, are a burden on researchers’ time and mental load. How then, can we help scientists adapt to this new way of working?

The key to changing practice lies in education and incentivisation.

Education alone is not enough to change behaviour but is useful to raise awareness. To back this up, relevant and effective incentives are needed to maintain engagement with sustainability. Incentivisation could take the form of intra- or inter-laboratory competitions, conference prizes, or by tying sustainability outcomes to funding opportunities.

The Freezer challenge

One example of a worldwide initiative is the Freezer Challenge, a competition promoting best freezer practices, which has recruited labs in over 27 countries so far.[11] This challenge originated in the USA, which is leading the way with assessments and initiatives to encourage institutions to be more environmentally friendly. Just by tackling this one piece of laboratory equipment, FreezerChallenge.org estimates that in 2022, participating laboratories they have saved 6,732 tCO2e – the equivalent energy use of 1,310 households (Figure 1).[11] Mitigating emissions from freezers is a particularly popular strategy in a sustainability manager’s arsenal because behavioural adjustments can in some cases slash energy expenditure by up to 30%. These energy savings can subsequently be converted to cost savings and used to build business cases for other sustainability improvements.

Motion And Sash Height (MASH) Alarms

While freezers are major contributors to the massive energy demand of research institutes, the main energy consumers in labs are usually HVAC equipment – in particular, fume hoods (refer to Table 1). Fume hoods are essentially large cupboards with an industrial hoover attached to the roof which pulls air up and away from the hood user, thus preventing them and others in the room from inhaling dangerous vapours. This air is filtered before being released elsewhere into the local environment. How hard the fume hood has to work depends on the sash height: the higher the sash, the wider the aperture, and therefore the fume hood has to extract to larger volumes of air to maintain the protective ventilating effect (this is termed ‘higher face velocity’ and is akin to trying to suck juice out of a carton using a straw with a wider diameter than the one supplied). Sash height is usually limited to further protect researchers from splashes, overflows and explosions, but these limits can be – and often are – overridden to allow equipment access. When the sash is closed, most fume hoods power down, thus consuming far less energy than fume hoods which are left even just slightly open.

To encourage researchers to close the fume hoods after working, ‘Shut the Sash’ stickers (or similar) have been estimated to cut HVAC energy consumption by around 70% – an energy saving worth $1,300 per hood annually.[10, 12] Automatic sash closers can save even more energy but, from personal experience, these devices are prone to misfiring, or even beeping at – and alarming – an unsuspecting scientist in the middle of a procedure. This represents a health and safety hazard and can lead to researchers consciously disengaging from sustainability; in one facility, the chemists became so fed up with the alarms that they jammed pencils in them to stop them working.[12] However, when fitted and used properly, they work extremely effectively, as shown by research carried out at the Massachusetts Institute of Technology (MIT) (Figure 2).[10]

Figure 2. Average sash height before and after installation of MASH alarms at MIT. Blue = MASH test group, n = 17. Red = control group, n = 19 (note: this group did have "Shut the Sash" stickers). It is not clear why the control group had a better baseline than the test group. Image courtesy of MIT Open Access Articles (CC BY).

Following these findings, motion and sash height (MASH) alarms are set to be fitted in all of MIT’s new buildings. This study also showed that in labs containing multiple fume hoods but with only one MASH alarm installed, there was also a significant reduction in average fume hood height.[10] The authors surmised that just the knowledge that the experiment was taking place influenced the behaviour of laboratory users, despite perhaps not having the alarm installed on their own hood. Anecdotally, this finding has been echoed in other locations. For this reason, it is important to keep on top of the ‘Shut the Sash’ messaging to prevent users reverting to bad practices.

Rebates from Facilities

At the University of Alabama Birmingham, labs that make efficiency improvements are given ‘credits’ by the Estates and Facilities Department. Institutions transitioning to more sustainable laboratory practices should find themselves saving money from reduced HVAC and electricity demands. These savings can be repurposed into financial rebates to help laboratories fund more efficient equipment, thereby creating more opportunities for the institution to save money. This model is already used at the University of Alabama Birmingham, where rebates have been used to upgrade ULT freezers to more efficient models. Since old ULT freezers can use up to 25 kWh/d, and the newest and most efficient ULT freezers consume 7 kWh/d, upgrading a ULT freezer could save an annual 6570 kWh. With today’s electricity prices (30p/kWh), this converts to a saving of £1,971. Therefore, if estates and facilities departments were to subsidise new freezers by £2,000, the return on investment would be just over one year.

In addition, institutions could use the money saved to invest in what is known as ‘beyond value chain mitigation’ – activities that fall outside the value chain which reduce or remove greenhouse gases from the atmosphere.[13] For example, institutions could invest in carbon removal credits (see Reuse for an explanation of advance market commitments). However, beyond value chain mitigation strategies should not be used as an excuse to avoid implementing concrete climate goals.

Accountability, Consistency & Transparency labels

Another way to make it easier for researchers to achieve their sustainability goals is to upgrade and refurbish certain equipment. A significant challenge in this respect is how to assess and compare the energy efficiencies of different laboratory equipment. To this end, the not-for-profit organisation My Green Lab has created a labelling system to help researchers identify the most environmentally friendly (or, more accurately, the least environmentally unfriendly) consumables and equipment. These are known as accountability, consistency and transparency (ACT) labels (Figure 3). There are already over 2,300 pieces of equipment that have ACT labels, and counting.[14] Manufacturers supply details of their equipment to an external consultant (SMS Collaborative), who translates this data into the ACT label. These labels are revalidated every two years and SMS Collaborative says that, so far, ‘scores are going down every time people recertify’.[15] Participating industry sources have confirmed that the ACT label has stimulated discussions about how their companies can make environmental improvements to their manufacturing processes. However, they also agree that one motivation for participating in the scheme is to drive sales with the additional marketing that the publication of this label provides. While the ACT labels are audited by an external consultancy, the 1 to 10 grading system is opaque. It is not clear, for example, if a company who makes a ‘manufacturing impact reduction’ in 2020, thereby receiving a ‘green’ score, also gets this ‘green’ score in 2022 upon revalidation, or whether the value is reset to ‘red’ (e.g. 10, the worst score) if no further improvements have been made since 2020. This would really drive innovation, but it would also be seen as unfair if the very same manufacturers that have helped to establish the programme were essentially penalised for having been early adopters. Furthermore, the My Green Labs model is part-funded by a multitude of equipment and consumable suppliers, many of whom also contribute their own data to create ACT labels for themselves. The ACT labels rely on the support of industrial backers and therefore there is a risk that these ACT labels are conduits for ‘greenwashing’. Certain brands have selectively completed ACT labels for equipment manufactured in high efficiency facilities, for example, which could potentially promote a false sense of security when buying other products from the same brand. This brand positioning provides a significant conflict of interest that may actually erode trust in the My Green Labs scheme. In addition, whereas you can normally find white papers or data commons articles for product lifecycle assessments, these are not available from My Green Labs. This raises the question: is the ACT scoring system deliberately opaque? Are ACT labels themselves accountable, consistent and transparent?

'My Green Lab' accreditation & ambassador program

At the 2021 United Nations Climate Change Conference (COP26), the My Green Lab Certification was hailed as a ‘key player to help pharmaceutical and medical tech companies achieve the goal of a zero-carbon world by 2050’. In the UN’s Race to Zero report, it states the aim that ‘95% of labs across major pharma and med-tech companies are My Green Lab certified at the green-level by 2030’ as a breakthrough outcome (i.e., an outcome that will drive system change by 2030).

My Green Lab has grown rapidly since the Covid-19 pandemic, presumably because the throwaway culture around personal protective equipment has opened people’s eyes to the wider problem of waste in healthcare and research settings. My Green Lab is run ‘for scientists, by scientists’. The messaging is persuasive and empowering (‘advocates/activists/ambassadors’, ‘building a global culture of sustainability in science’). The certification scheme offers tiered pricing for individuals depending on their position. However, if you want to carry out a My Green Lab certification for your laboratory, it will cost that laboratory hundreds of pounds. This is a significant barrier to entry for the UK market, because, at the moment, green lab programmes are run by altruistic students, who will struggle to secure funding – especially considering there is a very comprehensive, cheaper alternative in the UK (see below).

Progress in the UK

In the UK, there are few options for demonstrating environmental credibility in academia. Many of the tools used in the USA or in industry could be repurposed to provide the UK’s labs with useful tools to benchmark their laboratory sustainability. Indeed, AstraZeneca, which has committed to the goal of 98% decarbonisation of its scope 1 and 2 emissions by the year 2026, has won the Freezer Challenge repeatedly. Radical collaboration is required to repurpose USA-specific initiatives for the UK and, ideally bring them all under one, purpose-built umbrella for ease of use. We still have the opportunity to do this.

Founded in 2020, the Laboratory Efficiency Assessment Framework (LEAF), is a purpose-built platform run by University College London that can help institutions to build upon their environmental credentials ( Figure 4 ).[16] From my own personal experience, it is really easy to use.

If research funding bodies were to back LEAF or other laboratory sustainability initiatives awards on the policy level, there is little doubt that scientific and healthcare research would become more sustainable.

Indeed, a report in 2020 concluded that more research funders should trial policy interventions following the success of the Athena SWAN (Scientific Women’s Academic Network) accreditation, which caused the percentage of women granted funding to jump from 4% to 21% between the years of 2011 and 2016.[17]

Institutions and funding bodies should make it a priority to review the environmental credentials of laboratory equipment and make allowances for protected grant allocations in the name of improving sustainability. A similar type of equipment grant already exists in the form of the UKRI’s ‘core equipment awards’, which are used to increase research capacity of an institution.[18] Further to this, in instances where a laboratory is granted funding to increase its capacity, there should be a mechanism to allow the replaced equipment to be redistributed to smaller laboratories. To my knowledge, this type of competitive reallocation has not been established, although some lab equipment is occasionally sold second-hand. If done correctly, wherein the funding organisation takes back the used equipment and reallocates it (as opposed to letting the equipment gather dust or be disposed of unnecessarily) this could help to level up the scientific playing field (see: Wider Context of Research in the UK and Beyond).

Materials and supply chain

Materials

Re-source

Plastics are usually made from fossil fuels. There is considerable interest in alternative, non-fossil options from researchers and healthcare practitioners.[19] Laboratory consumables such as polypropylene centrifuge tubes made from majority recycled ‘bio-oils’, such as cooking oil, are entering the marketplace now.[23] Due to additional capital expenditure from the manufacturer, these more ecologically friendly alternatives may remain more expensive than the standard fossil fuel-derived equivalents for at least the short term future. Therefore, it is important that the purchase of more sustainable consumables is incentivised through rebates or tax discounts to allow demand for eco-friendly materials to build. In the long run, fossil plastics will become more expensive, and the demand for eco-plastics will scale up such that they can become cheaper.

There is scope for innovation in this space – for instance, by sourcing plastics from bio-attributable stocks. For example, Kelpi has just raised £3 million in seed funding to develop its seaweed-based packaging which is grease, acid, and water resistant.[24] Kelpi’s packaging is suitable for existing paper and cardboard recycling pathways and infrastructure and, furthermore, the company is partnering with food and beverage manufacturers to come up with materials that are stable to higher temperatures – this is essential if these plastics are to be used in labs.[25] In addition to thermal stability, within the laboratory there are additional challenges present for bio- attributable plastics: they must be waterproof (as opposed to water resistant), not interact at all with biological substances, be stable to high energy light exposures, and ideally they would be optically pure for microscopy purposes. Green Elephant Biotech has developed a tissue culture flask made from plant-derived polylactic acid (PLA) with an estimated 90% smaller carbon footprint than its equivalent fossil plastic-derived cell culture flask.[26] However, due to a lack of infrastructure, PLA is not currently widely recycled, so the single-use flasks are still typically sent for incineration (as all biohazardous waste currently is anyway – see Recycle), resulting in carbon dioxide release. Note that PLA is often referred to as ‘biodegradable’, when in fact the conditions required for it to biodegrade are almost never met.

Over time, to enhance environmental carbon dioxide removal, new petrochemical feedstocks such as CO2 and CH4 should be prioritised and invested in. Two UK-based innovators in this space are Econic Technologies and LanzaTech: both are developing catalytic methods of converting waste CO2 into a feedstock that could be used to make useful polymers (polycarbonates and polyethylene terephthalate, respectively). If these exciting technologies scale, they will also provide a CO2 drawdown mechanism that provides additional environmental benefit.

Reduce

Elimination or reduction approaches sit at the top of the waste management hierarchy. Since most of the carbon footprint of a product is generated by its manufacture and transport, substituting plastics with completely recyclable or reusable alternatives is the best way to reduce plastic waste. On a practical level, researchers may be amenable to experimental approaches aimed at reducing their materials consumption. For example, instead of making multi-component solutions every time they are needed, a master mix can be made to reduce the number of tubes and pipette tips used. This is good laboratory practice in any case because it reduces inter-experimental variability. In addition, there are some scenarios where reusable glassware or stainless steel can be used instead of single-use plastics. For instance, a lifecycle assessment of disposable acrylic vaginal specula versus stainless steel versions showed that the reusable stainless steel one had a lower carbon footprint than the disposable one after only two examinations had been completed. [27]

However, reduction schemes should not stand in the way of reproducibility, because having to repeat experiments increases the quantity of consumables used overall.
Reuse

Nowadays, certain laboratory practices, such as cell culture, rely entirely upon the provision of sterile single-use polystyrene flasks which are optically clear for microscopy, carry no risk of contamination, and they may even be coated with charged proteins to ensure optimal cell adhesion. Returning to glassware in this scenario is not a viable solution. In addition, plastic centrifuge tubes are irreplaceable, since they are specially designed for centrifuges which rely on having finely balanced rotors. While these items cannot be replaced or eliminated, they may possibly be reused. Dr Amy Pickering of the Roslin Institute in Edinburgh says that certain centrifuge tubes (Falcon tubes, in this instance) can be reused up to 50 times by rinsing, washing and autoclaving them. Over the course of a year, her lab alone is projected to save 20,000 Falcon tubes from landfill/incineration.[21]

The above example requires researchers to put their own time and effort into the washing and re-sterilising process. This is not the only solution, however. Moving to an ‘everything as a service’ (XaaS) business model could provide researchers with more eco-friendly, reused consumables at the convenience that they are accustomed to.[28] Although this subscription type service does not yet exist for laboratory equipment and consumables, the XaaS economic model clearly does exist (e.g. British toy rental service Whirli) and it is not impossible to envisage a future in which there is a circular economy for lab consumables.

XaaS models in which the manufacturer acts as the reuse service makes the manufacturer a ‘producer-owner’, thus incentivising the production of higher quality goods to improve reuse characteristics.

Supply Chain

Scope 3 emissions related to the procurement, transportation, post-processing and disposal of consumables are typically the largest contributor to emissions resulting from healthcare and scientific research. 9 In 2019, 62% of the NHS’s 25 megatonne carbon footprint was attributed to the supply chain – as compared to 24% originating from the direct delivery of care. [39] These indirect emissions are largely unaccounted for at present as there are no reporting incentives or legal standards for any of the involved parties (suppliers, researchers, universities, hospitals, waste handlers). However, industry is realising that it can get ahead of legislation and perhaps even shape policy itself by reporting its own emissions; environmental pledges and disclosures help them to maintain its reputation and boost consumer faith, yet maintain control over potentially sensitive, commercially relevant information. For example, in its quest to cut scope 3 emissions in half by 2030, AstraZeneca has devised a reporting template for their suppliers to confirm that they have ‘science-based targets’ to get them on track for net zero themselves.

How suppliers behave is not outside our control – purchasing directives can create implicit sustainability regulation. In particular, the NHS is the largest plastic user in Europe, and nearly a quarter (22%) of all NHS waste is plastic, which is reflective of the insatiable appetite for single-use plastics that we have come to accept.[6] Therefore, the NHS has the clout to exert significant pressure on suppliers by applying green criteria to its purchasing contracts. The Delivering ‘Net Zero’ National Health Service report echoes this need for pooled NHS purchasing power to enable sustainable procurement.[19] Indeed, NHS England has pledged to no longer purchase plastic stirrers and straws, expanded polystyrene, or single-use plastic cutlery (this only covers food suppliers for now).[40] We can ensure that non-innovative products and manufacturers will be gradually written out of the value chain by preferentially purchasing superior products.

Goods procurement in higher education is more fragmented, which is why there is a real need for purpose-built consortia to apply pressure on manufacturers. Understandably, researchers purchasing consumables tend to choose the cheapest product, rather than the ‘greenest’ – partly because they are responsible for their own grant spending, and also partly because at the moment there is little (if any) accessible information about the eco credentials of consumables. Most universities have a centralised purchasing marketplace that researchers use to order goods – this goes some way to help consolidate orders from suppliers where possible. Purchasing consortia such as the UK’s not-for-profit Southern Universities Purchasing Consortium (SUPC) have a role to play here by influencing what products are available to researchers on the procurement marketplace.

Aside from consortia, funding bodies can also influence spending habits in laboratories via their grant allocations. Since research funding is so competitive, this lever could itself be enough to persuade researchers to change their behaviour. Firstly, funding criteria should include a consideration of the environmental impact of the proposed research. Environmentally costly research should be scrutinised more carefully and/or be subject to special measures (e.g. enhanced reporting and/or demonstrable improvements over the funding period). Secondly, all grants should be allocated with ringfenced funding for laboratory sustainability initiatives, such as retrofitting autoclaves with water-saving devices, or a requirement to purchase more expensive equipment that has a longer lifespan than its cheaper alternative.

Forcing academics to put forth business cases for sustainable practices would increase awareness of the environmental problems of scientific research, change behaviour and initiate system change.

If we do not achieve system change, we will fail to meet the targets set out in the Paris Agreement, and climate change will become even more life threatening. Momentum is building for another legally binding United Nations treaty.[41] It is not too difficult to envisage a scenario in which, to meet ambitious climate targets and prevent a climate tipping point, governments have to step in to curb the actions of certain industries. This could result in the introduction of carbon budgets for activities such as scientific research. Therefore, it is important to future-proof by reducing our reliance on energy and resource-intensive practices. If carbon budgets are exceeded, offsets will have to be purchased at the expense of research grants. Decarbonising materials and the supply chain could yield important carbon credit savings.

When thinking about an issue that is as convoluted and opaque as supply chain emissions, it is important to channel the Churchillian principle of not letting ‘perfection be the enemy of progress’. There are no perfect or easy solutions to this problem – or else it would already have been solved. Incremental gains – small though they may seem – are infinitely better than zero progress!

Wider context of research in the UK and beyond

Conclusions

This report has discussed a broad range of strategies to tackle waste and inefficiency in the healthcare and laboratory sciences. In this report, I advocate for sustainability initiatives to be targeted to stakeholders based on their requirements and level of influence in the research landscape. Therefore, I have separated the main findings from my report into sections aimed at the key stakeholders: policymakers, funding bodies, universities/institutions, industry and researchers.

Recommendations for Policymakers

Due to the outsized demands for energy and consumables that originate from healthcare and laboratory spaces, the idea of research sustainability is gaining momentum among the international community. In practice, however, attempts to improve sustainability can be thwarted by misunderstood regulations and old frameworks that fail to consider the climate emergency and which hinder progress. A salient example of this is the handling of biohazardous waste: by law, nothing that has ever touched something ‘biohazardous’ can be recycled – despite the fact that many of these items could easily be decontaminated. This will hamper efforts to use chemical recycling as a means to divert research waste from landfill and incineration. Therefore, I advocate for system change brought about by new, purpose-built policies. This sentiment is also echoed in the EU’s Green Deal, which calls for a simplified regulatory environment.

One key tool to accomplish sustainability goals is the establishment of more accurate and reliable assessment tools to evaluate products and services. For instance, at the moment it is very difficult to assess environmental claims for so-called ‘greenwashing’, or to carry out lifecycle analyses on goods. While carbon footprint analyses vary according to the oil market among other things, it should be possible to establish reporting standards – but we cannot leave it to commercial groups alone to decide which figures they would prefer to report and those that they would like to leave out. Proposed targets must be validated by an independent body to ensure that greenwashing is avoided; the Science Based Targets initiative (SBTi) standard, and the Partnership for Carbon Transparency (PACT) framework seem promising in this respect. 13,34,54

Chemical recycling is seen by some as a complementary process to mechanical recycling, providing a step change towards creating a circular plastics economy. However, I think it is better to view chemical recycling as a method which deals with a separate waste stream that is currently non-recyclable and sent for incineration. That we, in the UK, continue to incinerate waste in built-up areas is horrifying. In addition to carbon dioxide, incineration produces toxic chemicals and particulate matter which have adverse health effects on the local population. 55,56 As discussed previously, chemical recycling could reduce our reliance on incineration. Since the plans for the first UK depolymerisation plant have been thrown into disarray by financial instability, the Government should support the establishment of chemical recycling as a waste stream. In addition, tax reliefs for recycled materials and higher taxes or quotas on the use of single-use or non-recyclable material could be effective policy interventions.

With regards to funding, the creation of ARIA is a big step in the right direction to cut the existing inefficiencies encountered in the acquisition of science funding. It seems that disruptive, innovative science is not a massive priority for the Government, but if, in the future, it becomes more of a priority (as it did during the Covid-19 pandemic), there are alternative funding models to pursue. For now, the Government should focus on policies that enable industrial scientists and business/venture capital to flow into laboratories so that more innovative ideas spin out commercially.

Finally, sustainability funding is currently classed as ‘non-essential aid spending’ – which is an issue because ‘non-essential’ spending is the first to be cut in times of hardship. Non-essential aid spending (including sustainability funding) has been in decline for three consecutive years now [2020-2023]. 57 This is short-sighted because the climate emergency is just that – an emergency – and cutting funding now results in loss of collaborative partnerships and talent that leaves us ill-equipped to tackle future challenges. In addition, to attract and retain talent in science and healthcare, it is imperative to pay the workers decently enough that they can live closer to where they work and support themselves and their families without having to find second jobs.

Recommendations for Funding Bodies

In the power dynamics of scientific research, funding bodies sit atop the pyramid. Together with policymakers, funding bodies possess incredible power to influence laboratories across the UK – and potentially even further afield. Science and healthcare research generate huge amounts of pollution and waste, which is harming our planet. Environmental health is implicitly linked to human health. Therefore, it is imperative that future research is done in a sustainable manner. Going forward, access to funding must be bound to credible and measurable sustainability improvements. LEAF awards (see: Progress in the UK) could be used to distinguish laboratories that are environmentally aware and making progress towards environmentally friendly research practices. The precedent for this type of incentive is found in the Athena SWAN awards, which has meaningfully increased gender equality in academia. Research sustainability is the next frontier for funding policy interventions.

On the individual level, funding bodies could reduce the impact of research by introducing sustainable procurement criteria or travel budgets putting limits on conference travel and/or encouraging environmentally conscious travel options. Increasing productivity and reducing inefficiencies is a win–win in increasing research sustainability and innovation. Productivity could be increased by increasing the duration and flexibility of funding. Innovation could be boosted by increasing researchers’ stipends to a liveable level and perhaps by allowing principal investigators to access sabbatical funding if they so required it. Finally, publishing metrics such as the h-index need to be phased out, as they contribute to over-publishing and self-citation, which leads to decreasing innovation (note: for the academics reading this, REF and TEF are outside of the scope of this report but I do sympathise).

Recommendations for Institutions

Change can also be brought about from within: the NHS, private hospitals, and universities must adapt quickly to a new way of thinking. Institutions are already moving towards sustainable energy procurement. Education and incentivisation are key to improving research efficiency and changing behaviour. Therefore, institutional sustainability teams and funding bodies each have important roles to play in influencing scientists. Institutions need to ensure that specialist laboratory teams are recruited to understand the needs of researchers, to implement green lab schemes, and to ensure that awareness remains high despite the relatively high turnover of students and staff typically seen in research labs and healthcare settings.

Permanent technicians focused on sustainability (e.g., to manage washing facilities or complete assessments and inventories) can help with this and should be employed to alleviate the burden on researchers and to increase consistency. Pooled technicians can also be funded by the UK Research and Innovation (UKRI) council. Central sustainability teams can also provide financial incentivisation by creating green rebates to distribute to energy- saving laboratories to allow them to invest in more efficient laboratory equipment.

Alongside institutional sustainability teams, funding agencies will play an influential role in applying pressure to grant applicants, who have jurisdiction over groups of researchers or even whole laboratories. Therefore, grant holders will be required to collaborate closely with institutional sustainability teams to ensure that they have oversight of their laboratory’s sustainability metrics.

In addition, university chancellors and other healthcare executives need to have skin in the game when it comes to the sustainability of their institutions. I propose that sustainability targets should be integrated into remuneration for university, healthcare, big pharma, and NHS leaders. 58 These sustainability metrics should be linked to performance-related pay or bonuses and any resulting penalties should be invested into sustainability initiatives.

On an institution-wide level, sustainability teams are over-promising and under-delivering on their zero-waste and zero-carbon goals. This is largely because the targets are unrealistic in the first place (see Leeds’ ‘Plastic- Free by 2023’)! 22 Cynically, one could hypothesise that these empty promises are nothing more than a public relations or advertising ploy, as students are very concerned about environmental issues. Institutions should take better responsibility for their wild sustainability targets, perhaps by using some of the frameworks and initiatives set out above (e.g., SBTi and PACT).

Dr Madeleine Luck

Throughout the course of my master’s degree and PhD, I have helped to develop pharmaceuticals, tested novel polymers, synthesised nanoparticles, and genetically engineered cells. Having been welcomed into all sorts of laboratories in different countries, I became frustrated at the wastefulness of certain types of laboratory research, and the lack of attention this issue has been given.

I hope that by bringing some of the ingrained, systemic issues in research practice into the light, we can make the necessary changes to ensure that scientific progress does notcome at the expense of the planet.
