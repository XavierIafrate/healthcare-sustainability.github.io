The environmental sustainability of science and healthcare research in the UK

Dr Madeleine Luck

Executive Summary

This report aims to introduce and contextualise the issue of laboratory sustainability in the UK. By surveying the current technologies and initiatives in the UK and beyond, the report shows that there is a slowly growing lab sustainability movement in the UK that requires accelerating.

By learning from some of the world’s leading laboratories and healthcare institutions in the USA and implementing changes quickly (within the next 5 years), the UK could drastically reduce its emissions relating to scientific and healthcare research. In turn, this would help the UK meet its net-zero goals.

This report clearly identifies concrete actions that the UK’s research agencies, funding bodies and researchers/healthcare providers need to take to support the transition to sustainable laboratory and healthcare practices. These recommendations are concisely summarised in the conclusions.

What's the problem?

Twenty years ago, few scientists – if any – cared about the sustainability of their laboratory practices.1 The most recent data available suggest that, in 2018, almost £4.8 billion was spent on health and medical research.2 This figure grows year on year. Most scientists would like to think that they are using grant money efficiently and that the benefits of their academic research outweigh any possible deleterious effects on the environment. Unfortunately, this is not always true. As the world’s population surpasses 8 billion and our energy requirements continue to consume an unsustainable amount of fossil fuels, it is important that everybody reviews their environmental impact. As scientists and healthcare workers, we are not simply pardoned because our work serves society – our research consumes a disproportionate amount of energy and generates huge volumes of waste.

Scientists have a far greater impact on the environment than the average citizen. A recent report from the Sustainable Healthcare Coalition estimated that healthcare research is responsible for 100 megatonnes of carbon dioxide equivalent (CO2e) emissions annually.3 Considering that there are 8.8 million scientists globally, and that approximately 12% of these are healthcare researchers, this means that the average healthcare scientist could have a carbon footprint of 100 tonnes per year.4 That’s a staggering 2,000 times more than the average Bangladeshi citizen.5 Considering the challenges that Bangladesh and other developing nations face with respect to the rising sea levels caused by climate change, this statistic crystallises, firstly, the injustice of the climate emergency, and secondly, that we scientists are more responsible for this inequality than we may have liked to think.

The NHS is responsible for a staggering 4% of the UK’s carbon emissions, and is the largest plastics user in all of Europe.6 There is no reason why we can’t attempt to reduce this impact while simultaneously upholding excellent levels of care. Good quality healthcare and reproducible science relies upon the provision of sterile equipment and consistent conditions. These typically take the form of single-use plastics and energy-guzzling heating, ventilation and air conditioning (HVAC) equipment.

Due to the complexity of the research ecosystem, the identification and quantification of areas for improvement is challenging. Meanwhile, funding bodies are slowly waking up to the idea that reducing inefficiencies in research is important from both a stakeholder and a financial perspective: grant holders need to learn to do more with less to (1) protect the environment and (2) ensure that funds are used effectively. In fact, the Medical Research Council recently announced a £1 million grant call seeking solutions to the problem of sustainability in life sciences and medical practice.7 However, at present there are no tangible incentives for science to be practised more sustainably.

The UK is planning to double scientific research investment – while simultaneously committing to a zero-carbon vision for 2050.8 As scientists and as stakeholders in planet Earth, how can we meet this challenge within our research institutions?

Systemic change is required.

In this report, I explain the challenges we face in our healthcare and research spaces, and explore innovative, translatable solutions to these problems from some of the world’s most forward-thinking organisations. Finally, I conclude with some recommendations for stakeholders in science and healthcare to allow our scientific community to reach a more sustainable future.

Facilities and how people use them.

Facilities

Research facilities and the energy that powers them are commonly referred to as scope 1 and scope 2 emissions, respectively.9 Scope 1 and 2 emissions can also be thought of as ‘direct’ emissions – those that are under the control of the organisation in question, as opposed to ‘indirect’ scope 3 emissions which occur as a consequence of other companies’ activities and are not controlled by the organisation. Research facilities are estimated to use 10 times more power per unit area than the average family home. In laboratories, HVAC equipment consumes between 40% and 70% of that energy.10 Therefore, HVAC equipment is clearly a priority target for energy-saving measures. Autoclaves and ultra-low temperature (ULT) freezers are other obvious targets. See Table 1 for a comparison of the relative energy demands of laboratory equipment. The future of research involves more automation and computing power – these machines will further add to the energy requirements of hospitals and research centres.

Dr Madeleine Luck

Throughout the course of my master’s degree and PhD, I have helped to develop pharmaceuticals, tested novel polymers, synthesised nanoparticles, and genetically engineered cells. Having been welcomed into all sorts of laboratories in different countries, I became frustrated at the wastefulness of certain types of laboratory research, and the lack of attention this issue has been given.

I hope that by bringing some of the ingrained, systemic issues in research practice into the light, we can make the necessary changes to ensure that scientific progress does not come at the expense of the planet.
