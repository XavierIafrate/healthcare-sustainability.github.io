About the Author

Executive Summary

This report aims to introduce and contextualise the issue of laboratory sustainability in the UK. By surveying the current technologies and initiatives in the UK and beyond, the report shows that there is a slowly growing lab sustainability movement in the UK that requires accelerating.

By learning from some of the world’s leading laboratories and healthcare institutions in the USA and implementing changes quickly (within the next 5 years), the UK could drastically reduce its emissions relating to scientific and healthcare research. In turn, this would help the UK meet its net-zero goals.

This report clearly identifies concrete actions that the UK’s research agencies, funding bodies and researchers/healthcare providers need to take to support the transition to sustainable laboratory and healthcare practices. These recommendations are concisely summarised in the conclusions.

What's the problem?

Twenty years ago, few scientists – if any – cared about the sustainability of their laboratory practices.[1] The most recent data available suggest that, in 2018, almost £4.8 billion was spent on health and medical research.2 This figure grows year on year. Most scientists would like to think that they are using grant money efficiently and that the benefits of their academic research outweigh any possible deleterious effects on the environment. Unfortunately, this is not always true. As the world’s population surpasses 8 billion and our energy requirements continue to consume an unsustainable amount of fossil fuels, it is important that everybody reviews their environmental impact. As scientists and healthcare workers, we are not simply pardoned because our work serves society – our research consumes a disproportionate amount of energy and generates huge volumes of waste.

Scientists have a far greater impact on the environment than the average citizen. A recent report from the Sustainable Healthcare Coalition estimated that healthcare research is responsible for 100 megatonnes of carbon dioxide equivalent (CO2e) emissions annually.3 Considering that there are 8.8 million scientists globally, and that approximately 12% of these are healthcare researchers, this means that the average healthcare scientist could have a carbon footprint of 100 tonnes per year.4 That’s a staggering 2,000 times more than the average Bangladeshi citizen.5 Considering the challenges that Bangladesh and other developing nations face with respect to the rising sea levels caused by climate change, this statistic crystallises, firstly, the injustice of the climate emergency, and secondly, that we scientists are more responsible for this inequality than we may have liked to think.

The NHS is responsible for a staggering 4% of the UK’s carbon emissions, and is the largest plastics user in all of Europe.6 There is no reason why we can’t attempt to reduce this impact while simultaneously upholding excellent levels of care. Good quality healthcare and reproducible science relies upon the provision of sterile equipment and consistent conditions. These typically take the form of single-use plastics and energy-guzzling heating, ventilation and air conditioning (HVAC) equipment.

Due to the complexity of the research ecosystem, the identification and quantification of areas for improvement is challenging. Meanwhile, funding bodies are slowly waking up to the idea that reducing inefficiencies in research is important from both a stakeholder and a financial perspective: grant holders need to learn to do more with less to (1) protect the environment and (2) ensure that funds are used effectively. In fact, the Medical Research Council recently announced a £1 million grant call seeking solutions to the problem of sustainability in life sciences and medical practice.7 However, at present there are no tangible incentives for science to be practised more sustainably.

The UK is planning to double scientific research investment – while simultaneously committing to a zero-carbon vision for 2050.8 As scientists and as stakeholders in planet Earth, how can we meet this challenge within our research institutions?

Systemic change is required.

In this report, I explain the challenges we face in our healthcare and research spaces, and explore innovative, translatable solutions to these problems from some of the world’s most forward-thinking organisations. Finally, I conclude with some recommendations for stakeholders in science and healthcare to allow our scientific community to reach a more sustainable future.

Facilities and how people use them.

Facilities

Research facilities and the energy that powers them are commonly referred to as scope 1 and scope 2 emissions, respectively.9 Scope 1 and 2 emissions can also be thought of as ‘direct’ emissions – those that are under the control of the organisation in question, as opposed to ‘indirect’ scope 3 emissions which occur as a consequence of other companies’ activities and are not controlled by the organisation. Research facilities are estimated to use 10 times more power per unit area than the average family home. In laboratories, HVAC equipment consumes between 40% and 70% of that energy.10 Therefore, HVAC equipment is clearly a priority target for energy-saving measures. Autoclaves and ultra-low temperature (ULT) freezers are other obvious targets. See Table 1 for a comparison of the relative energy demands of laboratory equipment. The future of research involves more automation and computing power – these machines will further add to the energy requirements of hospitals and research centres.

Since the pieces of equipment shown in Table 1 are most often shared between multiple groups and therefore serve between tens and hundreds of users, these facilities are difficult to manage efficiently. However, there are less obvious energy (and water) vampires, such as PCR machines, incubators, imaging devices and water baths, which all need attention too. Autoclaves, condensers and water distillers are also extremely water intensive, and there are opportunities to apply water-saving measures to these pieces of apparatus. Note that the scope of this report does not include the energy sourcing or efficiency of the buildings themselves; centralised teams must prioritise building and infrastructure improvements, as well as their transition to green energy. Although retrofits and new equipment do help to increase the efficiency of laboratory spaces, a lot can be achieved by changing how people use their facilities and equipment. To do this, we need to a) make people aware of the issues, and b) help them change their behaviour.

Behaviour

Every day, scientists make decisions about their research which inadvertently affect the environment. For instance, the average scientist would ordinarily not think twice about putting a thermocycler on overnight with a 4 oC hold, leaving the fume hood sash half open, or rummaging around in the ULT freezer for a minute too long. Some well- established and/or highly ingrained laboratory practices must be challenged in order to decrease the environmental impact of healthcare research. However, rules implemented unilaterally by laboratory managers can be surprisingly upsetting for researchers, who are often under a lot of pressure and feel constrained by time. Laboratory sustainability is undeniably inconvenient. For example, asking researchers not to run experiments overnight so that equipment can be shut down is not an empathetic solution; sometimes there isn’t enough time to run these experiments in the day and we must also recognise that scientists’ time is valuable. There is no ‘one size fits all’ key performance indicator (KPI) for sustainability in healthcare research. Different researchers and practitioners have unique requirements, and to understand these it is crucial that they are involved in decision-making. In my experience, I know that implementing sustainable science practices increased the amount of time I would typically spend on a task. Why? Washing reusable glassware, separating plasticware and taking waste to multiple different bins – the list of best practices goes on and includes (but is not limited to):

  • forward planning to ensure that equipment (e.g. dishwashers, autoclaves) is used efficiently
  • consolidating orders to ensure that procurement is as efficient as possible (see Materials and Supply Chain)
  • ensuring that inventories of fridge/freezer/cold room contents are created and kept up to date so that the doors are opened for as short a time as possible (therefore people are no longer rummaging around for missing/misplaced/mislabelled samples unnecessarily)
  • adjusting the setpoint of ULT freezers from -80 oC to -70 oC where necessary (reducing compressor cycles)
  • powering down equipment at the plug after use or fitting power timers on appropriate equipment
  • shutting down fume hoods in between use
  • not storing chemicals and equipment in fume hoods
  • recycling as much waste as possible (see Materials and Supply Chain)
These additional tasks, while menial, are a burden on researchers’ time and mental load. How then, can we help scientists adapt to this new way of working? The key to changing practice lies in education and incentivisation. Education alone is not enough to change behaviour. The consensus at the 2022 My Green Lab summit was that bespoke incentives are needed for researchers to change their scientific practice or engage with green lab activities. Incentivisation could take the form of intra- or inter-laboratory competitions, conference prizes, or by tying sustainability outcomes to funding opportunities. These initiatives must be kept fresh and relevant to maintain engagement. Case Study: Andrew King, the Health, Safety and Environment Director at AstraZeneca, detailed a successful engagement strategy in which his team encouraged researchers to identify their own areas of opportunity and constraint. By empowering researchers to identify their chosen KPIs, the AstraZeneca team ase able to gain high buy-in from researchers and assess where additional support was needed. The KPI dashboard showed scientists that they really were driving change within the organisation. Furthermore, by equating energy and emission savings to cost savings on the KPI dashboard, labs were able to compete with each other across different locations. This gamification resulted in AstraZeneca’s first ever 100% response rate to a sustainability initiative. Engagement is crucial because AstraZeneca is aiming to become carbon negative across its entire value chain by 2030 – a mammoth task! Somewhat unsurprisingly, free swag and awards are popular incentives for scientists. However, it is important to consider the needs of researchers before offering them things that they might not want. One of the most cost- effective and universally popular items to give out is, according to Nick Ciancio of the University of Alabama Birmingham, stickers! Stickers are extremely efficient tools because when designed in a concise, eye-catching way, they can provide effective prompts that change behaviour. Take, for instance, Shut the Sash fume hood stickers (to be discussed later). A bright design with the message ‘Be safe. Save energy. Shut the Sash’ stuck near the sash handle or fume hood light switch is effective and cheap. These stickers will save far more money than it costs to print them and hand them out. In addition, stickers provide free advertising – win-win! Behaviour can be influenced by incentives and policies, and there is evidence to show that researchers want science to be more environmentally friendly. The Million Advocates for Sustainable Science is a scientist-led group campaigning for top-down system change to ensure that financial resources from funding bodies are allocated to the most environmentally responsible and efficient laboratories. With 460 signatories (including me), this shows that a growing tide of scientists have an appetite for transformation in their own workplaces. However, implementing change can spook researchers due to the additional demands it invariably places on their time. It is important to introduce changes gradually. For instance, Ziyu Li of the University of Colorado Boulder describes a technique to build commitment to the Green Labs scheme by approaching researchers to sign a Green Labs pledge before gradually, over weeks and months, sending recommendations and checklists to improve environmental sustainability. Signatories to the pledge are posted on an attractive-looking poster to provide motivation to other labs. In addition, Green Lab awards (e.g., bronze, silver, gold) promote a positive culture around lab sustainability. Healthcare institutions in the USA are home to some of the most progressive sustainability initiatives. Because US hospitals are not taxpayer funded, they are subject to market forces, and therefore they must decide how best to attract patients. Often, they do this by becoming a ‘benevolent force’ in the community, for instance by putting on outreach events, sponsored activities, or undertaking research that is relevant to their local population. US institutions therefore understand that the health of individuals is intrinsically linked to the health of the surrounding environment and, as a result, there is much interest in institutional sustainability as a way to build trust with local communities. The Freezer challenge

One example of a worldwide initiative is the Freezer Challenge, a competition promoting best freezer practices, which has recruited labs in over 27 countries so far.11 This challenge originated in the USA, which is leading the way with assessments and initiatives to encourage institutions to be more environmentally friendly. Just by tackling this one piece of laboratory equipment, FreezerChallenge.org estimates that in 2022, participating laboratories they have saved 6,732 tCO2e – the equivalent energy use of 1,310 households (Figure 1).11 Mitigating emissions from freezers is a particularly popular strategy in a sustainability manager’s arsenal because behavioural adjustments can in some cases slash energy expenditure by up to 30%. These energy savings can subsequently be converted to cost savings and used to build business cases for other sustainability improvements

More than doubling the number of labs engaged in this year's Freezer Challenge represents a tipping point in a broader transition to sustainability across the science industry. The increase not only doubled the total amount of carbon saved but helped drive further awareness towards the growing green labs movement. Motion And Sash Height (MASH) Alarms

While freezers are major contributors to the massive energy demand of research institutes, the main energy consumers in labs are usually HVAC equipment – in particular, fume hoods (refer to Table 1). Fume hoods are essentially large cupboards with an industrial hoover attached to the roof which pulls air up and away from the hood user, thus preventing them and others in the room from inhaling dangerous vapours. This air is filtered before being released elsewhere into the local environment. How hard the fume hood has to work depends on the sash height: the higher the sash, the wider the aperture, and therefore the fume hood has to extract to larger volumes of air to maintain the protective ventilating effect (this is termed ‘higher face velocity’ and is akin to trying to suck juice out of a carton using a straw with a wider diameter than the one supplied). Sash height is usually limited to further protect researchers from splashes, overflows and explosions, but these limits can be – and often are – overridden to allow equipment access. When the sash is closed, most fume hoods power down, thus consuming far less energy than fume hoods which are left even just slightly open. To encourage researchers to close the fume hoods after working, ‘Shut the Sash’ stickers (or similar) have been estimated to cut HVAC energy consumption by around 70% – an energy saving worth $1,300 per hood annually.10,12 Automatic sash closers can save even more energy but, from personal experience, these devices are prone to misfiring, or even beeping at – and alarming – an unsuspecting scientist in the middle of a procedure. This represents a health and safety hazard and can lead to researchers consciously disengaging from sustainability; in one facility, the chemists became so fed up with the alarms that they jammed pencils in them to stop them working.12 However, when fitted and used properly, they work extremely effectively, as shown by research carried out at the Massachusetts Institute of Technology (MIT) (Figure 2).10

Figure 2. Average sash height before and after installation of MASH alarms at MIT. Blue = MASH test group, n = 17. Red = control group, n = 19 (note: this group did have “Shut the Sash” stickers). Following these findings, motion and sash height (MASH) alarms are set to be fitted in all of MIT’s new buildings. This study also showed that in labs containing multiple fume hoods but with only one MASH alarm installed, there was also a significant reduction in average fume hood height.10 The authors surmised that just the knowledge that the experiment was taking place influenced the behaviour of laboratory users, despite perhaps not having the alarm installed on their own hood. Anecdotally, this finding has been echoed in other locations. For this reason, it is important to keep on top of the ‘Shut the Sash’ messaging to prevent users reverting to bad practices.

Rebates from Facilities

At the University of Alabama Birmingham, labs that make efficiency improvements are given ‘credits’ by the Estates and Facilities Department. Institutions transitioning to more sustainable laboratory practices should find themselves saving money from reduced HVAC and electricity demands. These savings can be repurposed into financial rebates to help laboratories fund more efficient equipment, thereby creating more opportunities for the institution to save money. This model is already used at the University of Alabama Birmingham, where rebates have been used to upgrade ULT freezers to more efficient models. Since old ULT freezers can use up to 25 kWh/d, and the newest and most efficient ULT freezers consume 7 kWh/d, upgrading a ULT freezer could save an annual 6570 kWh. With today’s electricity prices (30p/kWh), this converts to a saving of £1,971. Therefore, if estates and facilities departments were to subsidise new freezers by £2,000, the return on investment would be just over one year. In addition, institutions could use the money saved to invest in what is known as ‘beyond value chain mitigation’ – activities that fall outside the value chain which reduce or remove greenhouse gases from the atmosphere.13 For example, institutions could invest in carbon removal credits (see REUSE for an explanation of advance market commitments). However, beyond value chain mitigation strategies should not be used as an excuse to avoid implementing concrete climate goals.

ACCOUNTABILITY, CONSISTENCY AND TRANSPARENCY LABELS

Another way to make it easier for researchers to achieve their sustainability goals is to upgrade and refurbish certain equipment. A significant challenge in this respect is how to assess and compare the energy efficiencies of different laboratory equipment. To this end, the not-for-profit organisation My Green Lab has created a labelling system to help researchers identify the most environmentally friendly (or, more accurately, the least environmentally unfriendly) consumables and equipment. These are known as accountability, consistency and transparency (ACT) labels (Figure 3). There are already over 2,300 pieces of equipment that have ACT labels, and counting.14 Manufacturers supply details of their equipment to an external consultant (SMS Collaborative), who translates this data into the ACT label. These labels are revalidated every two years and SMS Collaborative says that, so far, ‘scores are going down every time people recertify’.15 Participating industry sources have confirmed that the ACT label has stimulated discussions about how their companies can make environmental improvements to their manufacturing processes. However, they also agree that one motivation for participating in the scheme is to drive sales with the additional marketing that the publication of this label provides. While the ACT labels are audited by an external consultancy, the 1 to 10 grading system is opaque. It is not clear, for example, if a company who makes a ‘manufacturing impact reduction’ in 2020, thereby receiving a ‘green’ score, also gets this ‘green’ score in 2022 upon revalidation, or whether the value is reset to ‘red’ (e.g. 10, the worst score) if no further improvements have been made since 2020. This would really drive innovation, but it would also be seen as unfair if the very same manufacturers that have helped to establish the programme were essentially penalised for having been early adopters. Furthermore, the My Green Labs model is part-funded by a multitude of equipment and consumable suppliers, many of whom also contribute their own data to create ACT labels for themselves. The ACT labels rely on the support of industrial backers and therefore there is a risk that these ACT labels are conduits for ‘greenwashing’. Certain brands have selectively completed ACT labels for equipment manufactured in high efficiency facilities, for example, which could potentially promote a false sense of security when buying other products from the same brand. This brand positioning provides a significant conflict of interest that may actually erode trust in the My Green Labs scheme. In addition, whereas you can normally find white papers or data commons articles for product lifecycle assessments, these are not available from My Green Labs. This raises the question: is the ACT scoring system deliberately opaque? Are ACT labels themselves accountable, consistent and transparent?

Conclusions

Dr Madeleine Luck

Throughout the course of my master’s degree and PhD, I have helped to develop pharmaceuticals, tested novel polymers, synthesised nanoparticles, and genetically engineered cells. Having been welcomed into all sorts of laboratories in different countries, I became frustrated at the wastefulness of certain types of laboratory research, and the lack of attention this issue has been given.

I hope that by bringing some of the ingrained, systemic issues in research practice into the light, we can make the necessary changes to ensure that scientific progress does not come at the expense of the planet.
